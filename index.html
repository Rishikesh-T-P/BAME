The "Melting Ice Cream" Problem

Imagine an ice cream shop where the ice cream starts melting the moment you enter the queue. If the line is short, you get a frozen treat. If the line is long, you get a puddle.

Quantum bits (qubits) behave similarly. They suffer from decoherence over time. When we process qubits sequentially (like in a queue), they wait. While they wait, they rot.

Design Implications

Conclusions for Quantum Network Architects

1

Don't Maximize Throughput

In classical networks, we push $\lambda \to \mu$. In quantum networks, this is fatal. The "Sweet Spot" $\lambda^*$ is often significantly lower than the server rate to prevent queue buildup and subsequent decoherence.

2

Kill the Jitter

Deterministic processing times ($M/D/1$) are strictly better than variable ones. Quantum gates and detectors should be designed for consistency rather than just average speed.

3

Side Information Helps

Proposition 1 highlights that if the receiver knows the exact arrival/departure times, capacity increases. Timing information effectively removes uncertainty about how much "rot" occurred.

4

Beyond Quantum

These models apply to any system with "impatience" or deadlines, such as Crowdsourcing (workers get bored waiting) or Real-time IoT (data becomes stale).
